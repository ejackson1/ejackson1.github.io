Executive Summary

Mobile robotic bases provide mobility for robotic serial manipulators and are essential for service technologies, warehouse applications, and robotics research. However, current mobile bases are expensive and require significant effort to integrate with robotic manipulators. The goal of this Major Qualify Project (MQP) was to design and implement a modular, programmable, and affordable mobile platform for interfacing with various manipulators. The team created a chassis and elevator system to increase mobility and introduce effortless electrical and mechanical integration with various manipulators utilizing a modular design. Furthermore, abstraction between user input and manipulator-specific hardware drivers was developed for seamless control of the base and manipulator.

Modular Insert

The Modular Insert (MI) was designed to house the Controller, a necessary computer for controller many different robotic arms, and plug into place to the main robot with the Universal Plug. This functionality enables any robotic arm to be mounted onto the MI and interface with the Mobile Base. We designed a cut-out in the Mobile Base to be able to fit a maximum size of 18.7" x 16.7" x 10.6" (the UR5e controller). The MI also can physically mount any robotic arm and has handles for easy transportation. Finally, the wings on the MI are used to mount onto the Home Base, a charging station.

Elevators - Pivoting and 24" Vertical Translation

3 Scissor Jacks enable for elevation of the robotic arm and MI. Each scissor jack is individually actuated and controlled with a P controller to ensure accurate altitude translation. By keeping 1 or more scissor jacks stationary, the entire tabletop can pivot. In practice, we use pivoting to reach into obscure areas.

Reliability and Safety

The Battery Management System (BMS) provides overcurrent, overcharge, and undercharge protection while balancing individual battery cells. The limit switches prevented the elevators from overcompressing and guaranteed a reliable home position. Our robot was constructed in Gazebo, a simulation software, to verify untested code. It demonstrated modularity and abstraction between arms.
