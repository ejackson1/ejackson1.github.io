Executive Summary

Note: Video is 4x Speed
Quadruped walking robots draw inspiration from various mammals and insects, incorporating bio-inspired mechanisms that improve both stability and the range of movement. This, in turn, expands their capability to access diverse terrains. However, these advancements demand heightened precision in path planning and kinematic calculations.

The objective of this project is to create a model and simulation for the motion of a symmetrical quadruped walking robot, complete with shoulder, elbow, and knee joints. This objective is achieved by solving both forward and inverse kinematics for the model while exploring different gait patterns.

Kinematic Calculations

The initiation of the robot's kinematics involved the essential step of defining the duty factor. This entailed the development of kinematic phase diagrams for a quadruped robot employing a symmetric forward walking gait while varying the duty factors. During the analysis of these diagrams, we scrutinized whether any of the leg's transfer phases coincided with one another. Such overlap would result in the situation where two or more legs are simultaneously lifted off the ground, a scenario that could greatly heighten instability, especially for a quadruped robot.

Upon examination, it was determined that when employing a duty factor of 0.7, there was a brief 1/10th phase overlap in the transfer phase between legs two and three. However, by increasing the duty factor to 0.75, none of the transfer phases showed any overlap. With this adjustment, as soon as one leg made contact with the ground, the subsequent leg began to lift off, ensuring a smooth and continuous motion. As a result, a duty factor of 0.75, coupled with a symmetric forward walking gait, was chosen as the optimal configuration for this project.

Kinematic Phase Diagrams with a Duty Factor of 0.7 (left) and 0.75 (right)

Trajectory planning of the foot tip is broken down into six points to form the path of the foot. The spacing of the points was determined by the input velocity, stride length, and cycle period. These points in relation to the ground coordinate system so a mapping is completed to have these points with respect to the robot's body coordinate system. Inverse kinematics are then calculated to obtain the necessary joint angles by considering each leg as individual robot arms with three joints (the hip, knee, and ankle).

The calcualted angles to reach the desired 6 waypoints are then relayed into ROS publishers at a specific time. We loop this 10 times to have the robot walk straight for 10 cycles.
