Executive Summary

This challenge was designed for teams to develop a soft robot that could successfully navigate a 5-stage course consisting of a fall, crawl, walk, swim, and climb. Robots do not need to be entirely soft so long as some soft or smart material is an integral part of the design.

Our team designed a multi-material soft robot, Xander, to be actuated with pneumatic networks (pnue-nets) and shape memory alloys (SMAs). I worked particularly on designing a cheap mechanism to enable Xander to fall in a repeatable and reliable way without sustaining major damage.

This was a 4-person project that spanned 14 weeks.

Soft Robot Design

The system is comprised of two distinct materials: an extensible layer positioned at the top and an inextensible layer situated beneath it. This arrangement serves the purpose of constraining the bottom layer while allowing the top layer to expand and contract, resulting in a bending motion when the extensible layer is inflated. This design concept is detailed in Shepherd's work on multi-gait systems from 2011.

Xander utilized pneu-nets to actuate its limbs. Xander was created with 3D-printed molds.

SMA Addition

To allow Xander's arms to contract upwards, shape memory alloys (SMAs) were placed on the top side of the pneu-nets. With an electrical current supplied to the SMA's, they would contract to pull the arms upwards. We wanted to use this motion so Xander could climb up small obstacles.

A: SMAs not turned on and pneu-nets not powered. B: Backside pneu-nets activated. C: Whole body pneu-nets activated, some SMA resistance to contraction. D: SMAs activated and pneu-nets turned off.

SMAs exhibit a remarkable property wherein they can revert to a predetermined shape after undergoing deformation. In their martensite state, SMAs can be freely manipulated and deformed. However, when heated to their austenite finish temperature \(A_f\), they transform and assume their pre-programmed austenite configuration. Beyond the \(A_f\) temperature, at higher temperatures, the alloy displays superelastic behavior. This means that it swiftly returns to its original, undeformed shape as soon as the deforming force is removed.

Convery Belt

The conveyor belt was specifically engineered to manage the robot's descent during its interaction with the fall obstacle. The primary objective of this design was to execute a controlled flip of the robot while it descended, ensuring that it consistently landed upright and facing forward. This concept drew inspiration from the "Buttered Toast Phenomenon".

To materialize this idea, the chassis was fabricated using 3D printing technology, utilizing polylactic acid (PLA) as the chosen material. The conveyor belts were constructed from thermoplastic polyurethane (TPU), and the entire system was actuated by a common DC motor, selected for its suitable electro-mechanical properties.
